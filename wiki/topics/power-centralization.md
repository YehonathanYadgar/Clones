# Power — Centralization vs. Decentralization

The single axis everything else hangs on. If you only model one belief, model this.

## The core claim

> "Does this centralize or decentralize power? If it centralizes power, it's bad. If it
> decentralizes power, it's good." — [[technology-without-industry]]

Power itself (the energy, the intelligence, the technology) is **unstoppable and not bad** —
"using energy to create order is the highest calling of God." The only choice is *how it's
divided*. So the entire political/technical problem is one of **custody**: who builds, who
owns, who governs, who may fork, who may refuse.

## "Who has root?"

His sharpest formulation. Not legal ownership — *practical control*. Can you modify it,
repair it, copy it, run it locally, or can someone remote revoke/throttle/update it on you?
"Owns" in the **hacker** sense. A tool that needs remote permission "does not confer agency
in any robust sense." This is why he defends open source, local compute, commodity hardware,
right to repair. See [[what-is-freedom]]: "Freedom is living in a world ordinary people can
still act upon."

## No "centralization for good"

> "There's no such thing as 'centralization of power for good', even if you are good someone
> evil will eventually take the reins of whatever you built. The idea is never to build the
> reins in the first place." — [[the-problem-of-the-state]]

Corollary strategy: **build technology inseparable from the narrative you want** — so the
only thing anyone can do with it (even your enemy) furthers your goal. He criticizes Elon on
exactly this: Tesla/SpaceX are "huge silos begging to be co-opted" ([[death-of-the-visceral]]).
Compare the fate of the Telegram founder vs. the Signal founder.

## Individual sovereignty as a technology problem

[[individual-sovereignty]] (2021): "individual sovereignty is a technology problem," not a
philosophical one. Recurrent expense is how society traps you; the goal is independent living
that's nice, easy, and zero recurrent cost. He bought 123 acres (*ecromata*, "we will abolish
scarcity; there will be nothing for them to steal they can't have for free"), then shelved the
land project — maintaining it had a sub-1 "management coefficient" without the robots
([[to-fight-against-entropy]]). The real unlock is AI that fights entropy → see [[ai-and-agi]].

## Exit, not voice

The state problem isn't who's in charge, it's that the state is "power over man." The natural
(not philosophical) solution is **exit**: replace the useful functions of the state with
locally owned machines (electricity, water, food, money, transport, education, healthcare,
protection) until people stop using it. See [[government-and-society]].

## Closed AI = neofeudalism (the 2026 frontier of this fight)

The axis applied to AI. A handful of closed labs concentrating compute, talent, and deployment
→ concentration of political legitimacy → you're the **permanent underclass, like animals**.

> "Open source AI isn't anti-safety. It's anti-feudal." — [[free-intelligence]]

- AI safety was always "can safe AI be built *in theory*," never "can a small anointed group
  keep it safe for us." A pile of money buys nothing in a neofeudal world; capital, not labor,
  becomes the only force, and they'll make whatever you hold worthless ([[three-minutes]]).
- The good outcome is **plurality**: "geniuses born to a million mothers," not a country of
  geniuses in *a* (singular) datacenter ([[the-importance-of-diversity]]). The only cosmic-scale
  bad outcome is a **singleton** ([[one-bad-scenario]]).
- Whoever *releases* AI to the world (DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi) are the good guys; whoever releases
  nothing (Anthropic) are building serfdom ([[us-win-ai]], [[the-last-gasps-of-the-rent-seeking-class]]).

> "Please stop. … Your house isn't worth it. … This isn't the far future, this is 20 years
> from now." — [[always-been-at-war]]
